The Story So Far

What we just covered

The talk walked through the research that proved text contains tradeable financial information. Here's the arc.

07

Tetlock proved word counts predict markets

By scoring daily Wall Street Journal columns with a simple word list, Paul Tetlock showed that newspaper pessimism predicted next day market declines and trading volume spikes. No machine learning. Just counting negative words.

11

Loughran & McDonald fixed the dictionary

The general purpose dictionary Tetlock used misclassified roughly 75% of "negative" financial words. Words like liability, risk, and cost aren't negative in finance. Loughran and McDonald built domain specific word lists from scratch, and that's what you'll use today.

Now you replicate it with better tools

You have real financial articles, the actual Loughran McDonald word lists, and real market data. In 20 minutes, you'll build the same pipeline those researchers built, but with the improved dictionary. Let's go.

The Core Formula
Sentiment = (positive − negative) ÷ total words
That's it. Score each article, aggregate by month, and compare to the market.
Workshop Instructions

Six steps, twenty minutes

The starter notebook walks you through each step. Here's what you're doing and why.

01

Load the data

Open the starter notebook. Load the financial articles and the two Loughran McDonald word lists (positive and negative). If you're using Colab, upload the files first.

02

Read before you code

Open one or two articles and actually read them. Scroll through the negative word list. Get a feel for the language. Financial text is measured. Small differences in tone carry meaning.

💡 This step matters more than you think. Students who skip it always struggle later.
03

Score each article

Tokenize the text, count how many words appear in the positive list and the negative list, then compute the sentiment score: (pos − neg) / total.

💡 Don't forget to lowercase your text. The word lists are all lowercase. If your scores are all near zero, that's correct. Financial text is subtle.
04

Aggregate by month

Group your article scores by month using a pandas groupby. This gives you a monthly sentiment time series.

05

Plot sentiment vs. market data

Merge your monthly sentiment scores with the provided market volatility data. Plot them together. Look for any visual relationship.

06

Interpret your results

Compute the correlation between your sentiment signal and market volatility. Did you find a relationship? Positive or negative? Does it match what Tetlock found?

💡 If you didn't find a signal, that's a result too. Ask yourself: is the dataset too small? Would daily aggregation work better? In research, understanding why something doesn't work teaches you more than finding something that does.

Key Concepts

Ideas to keep in mind

As you work through the exercise, these are the big takeaways from today's session.

Context blindness

"Not profitable" contains a positive word. Bag of words can't detect negation or sarcasm. It's the simplest possible method, and it still finds signal. Imagine what smarter methods can do.

Weak signals have value

If your signal is right 52% of the time over a thousand trades, that's a profitable edge. In quantitative finance, you don't need to be right every time. You need to be right slightly more often than wrong, at scale.

Domain dictionaries matter

Generic tools like VADER or TextBlob will give you worse results on financial text. The lesson is universal: off the shelf NLP fails in specialized domains unless you adapt it.

Correlation ≠ Causation

Does the newspaper's tone predict the market, or does the market's performance predict the newspaper's tone? Disentangling causality in time series data remains one of the field's hardest open problems.
